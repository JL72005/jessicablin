Research

For my CS315 class (Data Science and the Web), I and three other students developed a research project analyzing the use of language and advertising in the beauty and skincare industries on TikTok and YouTube. We used webscraping to obtain data from over 15000 TikTok and YouTube vidoes, and then used LDA and BERT to find topic groupings, manually labeled the topics, and then used setFit to retest videos to verify our accuracy of the labels. We found that before 2020, the languaged used in videos was targeted towards children while that category disappeared in videos created after 2020. Product promotion changed from ebing explicitly mentioned to being incorporated subtly so that the viewer does not know whether or not the video content is sponsored.

For my ECON203 class (Econometrics), I and two other students analyzed a minimum drinking age laws data set from 1980 and the 2000 US population census to study the effects of MLDA on college educational attainment. We employed a fixed effects model for the analysis, and we found that employing a minimum drinkage law increased the likelihood of a person obtaining some college education by 4.2% and that effect was more pronounced for men rather than women.

In PSYC 310R (Research Methods in Psychology), I and three other students conducted a research project on the effects of perceived similarity and domain of similarity on roommate satisfaction, self-esteem, and reading comprehension. we conducted a 2x3 between-subjects factorial experiment involving the manipulation of heightened perceived similarity and domain of similarity. Initial analysis of two-way ANOVA results suggest that domain of similarity rather than perceived similarity accounted for differences between participants' scores of relationship satisfaction. Specifically, those in the recreation habits condition reported significantly lower relationship satisfaction scores relative to the recreation and reading skills. These results actually contradict prior work which noted recreation habits as being a predictor for increasing similarity (Martin and Anderson, 1995). An explanation for this could be due to inherent differences that prevented making similarity salient for participants. Thus, it demonstrates the need for more work in finding ways to effectively manipulate perceived similarity especially within a roommate context.

In order to explore the factors that affect divorce, we found the Split or Stay: Divorce Predictor Data Set from Kaggle that consisted of 170 observations of divorce and married couples with a binary response variable of divorce or married based on answers to 54 statements regarding different aspects of the relationship. To analyze the data we took two different approaches – linear models and tree methods. From our results, we can see that the linear and tree models appear to perform equally well. Conducting principal component analysis (PCA) and examining the first two principal components revealed that married and divorced couples form two visually distinct clusters, explaining why each of the models were able to accurately predict the marital status of the given couple even though the predictors incorporated in the models varied.
